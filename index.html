An Alternative Syllabus

On Becoming More Fully Yourself

A self-directed curriculum for a South Indian woman in Silicon Valley — across psychology, philosophy, technology, music, spirituality, and feminism.

Level: Intermediate, wide-ranging Format: Mixed media Goals: Depth · Connection · Wisdom Horizon: Lifelong

This syllabus is not organized by discipline. It is organized by questions — the kind that don't resolve but deepen. It moves between ancient and contemporary, Indian and Western, technical and contemplative, always looking for the thread that connects them. Work through it however suits you: sequentially, by mood, or by following your own tangents.

01

The Mind That Listens — Music, Cognition & Consciousness

Psychology · Philosophy · Hindustani Classical
You have lived inside Hindustani classical music your whole life. This module asks: what is actually happening — neurologically, philosophically, spiritually — when you listen, practice, or improvise? It takes your existing knowledge seriously as a site of inquiry.
Book
This Is Your Brain on Music
Daniel J. Levitin
A neuroscientist and former record producer explains how music is processed in the brain. Read it critically — his examples are Western-centric, but the cognitive science is solid. Ask: what does he miss about raga grammar and time cycles?
Book
The Ragas of North India
Walter Kaufmann
Dense and encyclopedic, but worth dipping into. Use it as a reference alongside your practice — seeing the structure you already know described analytically can be revelatory.
Book
Music and the Mind
Anthony Storr
A Jungian psychiatrist examines why music matters so profoundly to human experience. Explores music as a developmental, emotional, and spiritual necessity — not mere entertainment.
Paper
"Music and Emotion: Theory and Research"
Juslin & Sloboda (eds.) — Oxford University Press, 2001
A foundational anthology. The chapter on "emotional contagion" is particularly striking — it explains why a certain alap can feel like grief you didn't know you had. Freely available in many university libraries.
Paper
"Raga as Archetype: Structure and Affect in North Indian Classical Music"
Various ethnomusicological journals — search JSTOR
Search for raga + affect or rasa theory on JSTOR. The literature on how ragas are thought to embody emotional and temporal states (rasa theory) connects directly to both aesthetics and psychology.
Video
Lecture Series: "The Biology of Music" — Robert Zatorre, Montreal Neurological Institute
Available on YouTube / TED
Zatorre's research on music and dopamine is transformative. His experiments on "chills" during music — and why some people experience them and others don't — will change how you hear your own reactions to a particularly beautiful meend.
Music
Raga Yaman — Pandit Bhimsen Joshi (Any live recording)
Archival recordings, ITC Sangeet Research Academy
Listen not as a student, but as an analyst. Where does time dilate? Where does the raga breathe? Pair with your reading on music cognition and notice what theory can and cannot explain.
The connecting thread The concept of rasa — emotional essence as aesthetic experience — predates Western affective science by two millennia. Bharata Muni's Natyashastra (c. 200 BCE) is one of the earliest systematic psychologies of emotional experience. Consider reading the relevant chapters alongside Levitin.
02

The Examined Self — Psychology, Spirituality & Inner Life

Psychology · Spirituality · Philosophy of Mind
You live between frameworks — the rationalist, quantified culture of Silicon Valley and a tradition of spiritual practice that refuses to be measured. This module refuses to resolve that tension. It sits inside it.
Book
The Embodied Image: Imaginative Healing Practices and Contemplative Psychology
Jerome Bernstein
Examines how imagination, body, and psyche intertwine — and how Western psychology systematically suppresses modes of knowing that non-Western traditions center. Directly relevant to your experience navigating both.
Book
The Untethered Soul
Michael A. Singer
Accessible entry into Advaita-adjacent consciousness studies. Less rigorous than other texts here, but unusually good at articulating the observer-self distinction. A useful bridge between pop-psych and deeper philosophy.
Book
Waking Up: A Guide to Spirituality Without Religion
Sam Harris
Read Harris as a Western rationalist genuinely trying to account for meditative experience without metaphysics. Then read the critiques — particularly from Buddhist scholars like B. Alan Wallace — to see what he flattens. The debate between them is more interesting than either position alone.
Book
The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali (commentary edition)
Swami Satchidananda (trans. & commentary)
The classical psychology of the Indian tradition. The Sutras are a systematic map of mental processes — citta vritti (fluctuations of the mind) is more rigorous as a cognitive framework than many modern accounts. Read with modern psychology in mind.
Book
The Psychology of Religion and Coping
Kenneth I. Pargament
Rigorous empirical psychology examining how spiritual practices actually function as coping mechanisms — not dismissively (as mere illusion) but as genuine psychic events with measurable effects. Important counterweight to secular reductionism.
Video
"The Hard Problem of Consciousness" — David Chalmers, TED
TED Talks / YouTube
The best 18-minute introduction to why consciousness is philosophically devastating for materialist science. Chalmers doesn't resolve it — nobody does — but he frames the question with beautiful clarity. Essential context for any serious engagement with spirituality and psychology.
Paper
"Altered States of Consciousness" (1969)
Charles Tart — Journal of Transpersonal Psychology
A foundational paper in transpersonal psychology. Tart argues rigorously that different states of consciousness are not simply "more or less" awake — they are qualitatively different systems. Reframes what contemplative traditions have always claimed.
Podcast
On Being — with Krista Tippett
onbeing.org
Start with: the episodes with Bessel van der Kolk (trauma and the body), Robin Wall Kimmerer (indigenous knowledge and science), and V.V. Raman (Hindu physicist on science and spirituality). The archive is vast — use search to find the intersections that matter to you.
The connecting thread The split between "rational" and "spiritual" is a product of specific Western intellectual history — particularly the Enlightenment and the mind-body dualism of Descartes. Indian epistemological traditions (pramana in Sanskrit) never made this split. Tracking that difference is itself a form of intellectual liberation.
03

The Machine & The Human — Technology, Society & Power

Sociology of Technology · Tech Criticism · Philosophy of Technology
You work inside the machine. This module helps you see it from the outside — not to leave it, but to understand what you are part of. The best tech criticism is not anti-technology. It is anti-naïve.
Book
The Age of Surveillance Capitalism
Shoshana Zuboff
The definitive sociological account of how behavioral data became the raw material of a new economic logic. Dense and demanding, but repays close reading. This book will permanently change how you see the products you work on.
Book
Invisible Women: Data Bias in a World Designed for Men
Caroline Criado Perez
Meticulously evidenced account of how the default human in data, product design, medicine, and urban planning is male — and the costs of that invisibility. As a woman building tech, this is both professional and personal reading.
Book
Race After Technology
Ruha Benjamin
Introduces the concept of the "New Jim Code" — how algorithmic systems reproduce racial inequity under the guise of neutrality. Essential reading for anyone building products at scale. Connects beautifully to questions of whose data, whose values, whose future.
Book
The Question Concerning Technology
Martin Heidegger (essay, 1954)
Heidegger's argument that modern technology is not a tool but a way of seeing — a mode of revealing the world as "standing reserve" for exploitation. Difficult, but the central insight is foundational to all serious tech philosophy. Read with a commentary if needed.
Essay
"A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985)
Donna Haraway — Socialist Review, now widely anthologized
One of the most important essays of the 20th century. Haraway argues for the cyborg as a figure that refuses the binaries of nature/culture, human/machine, self/other. Transgressive, joyful, and rigorous. Free online.
Video
The Social Dilemma
Netflix documentary, 2020
Yes, you've probably heard of it. Watch it anyway — specifically for Tristan Harris's account of "persuasive technology" design. Then watch the critiques of it (search: "The Social Dilemma critique") to get the full picture. It is both correct and incomplete.
Paper
"Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980)
Langdon Winner — Daedalus, Vol. 109
Eight pages that changed how the humanities thinks about technology. Winner argues that technologies embed political values and power relations — they are never neutral. Freely available online. Read it in one sitting.
Podcast
Tech Won't Save Us — Paris Marx
Available on all podcast platforms
Consistently rigorous left-critical tech analysis. Best episodes: interviews with Safiya Umoja Noble (search bias), Kate Crawford (AI and power), and Ben Tarnoff (tech and labor). Not ideologically monolithic — guests genuinely disagree.
Website
Algorithmic Justice League — ajl.org
Founded by Joy Buolamwini
Research, advocacy, and accessible explainers on bias in AI systems. Joy Buolamwini's work on facial recognition accuracy disparities (across race and gender) is landmark. Her TED talk is also excellent. The site bridges academic research and activist practice.
The connecting thread The myth of technological neutrality is also a story about whose worldview gets baked in as the default. As a South Indian woman in Silicon Valley, you are professionally and personally positioned to see this asymmetry from the inside. That perspective is an epistemological asset, not just a personal experience.
04

Feminist Thought & The Life We Actually Live

Feminism · Intersectionality · Philosophy · Sociology
Feminist theory at its best is not a political position — it is an epistemology. It asks: who gets to produce knowledge, about whom, for whose benefit? This module moves from foundational texts to the questions closest to your own life: migration, career, body, care work, and what it means to be "successful" on terms not designed for you.
Book
Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center
bell hooks
hooks argues that feminist theory built from the experience of the most marginalized women — not the most privileged — produces better, more truthful theory. As someone who navigates multiple margins simultaneously (race, gender, immigration), this framing will feel clarifying.
Essay
"Mapping the Margins: Intersectionality, Identity Politics, and Violence Against Women of Color" (1991)
Kimberlé Crenshaw — Stanford Law Review
The essay that introduced the concept of intersectionality as a legal and analytical framework. Crenshaw shows how Black women fall through the gaps between race-discrimination law and sex-discrimination law — because neither accounts for both at once. Free online.
Book
The Second Sex
Simone de Beauvoir
Don't try to read it all at once. Start with the introduction ("One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman") and Part IV. De Beauvoir's framework of woman as "Other" — defined in relation to the male default — prefigures everything from Haraway to Criado Perez.
Book
Brotopia: Breaking Up the Boys' Club of Silicon Valley
Emily Chang
A reported account of Silicon Valley's culture of sexism. Read it not as a revelation but as documentation — the names and companies are familiar. Useful as sociology alongside the theoretical texts.
Book
Unaccountable: A Memoir of Corruption, Compromise, and the Fight Against Injustice (or: Weapons of Math Destruction)
Cathy O'Neil
Weapons of Math Destruction by O'Neil is the better recommendation here — she examines how opaque algorithmic models punish the already disadvantaged under the pretense of objectivity. Direct, clear, essential reading for someone in tech.
Book
Feminism Without Borders: Decolonizing Theory, Practicing Solidarity
Chandra Talpade Mohanty
Mohanty, an Indian feminist scholar, critiques Western feminism's tendency to construct a monolithic "Third World Woman" and argues for a feminism that is genuinely transnational. As a South Indian woman who has lived both contexts, her analysis will resonate in specific ways.
Video
"The Urgency of Intersectionality" — Kimberlé Crenshaw, TED
TED.com / YouTube
Crenshaw makes her academic framework visceral and immediate through real case studies. One of the best TED talks ever made. 19 minutes.
The connecting thread The Indian feminist tradition — including figures like Savitribai Phule, Tarabai Shinde, and later Kumari Jayawardena — has its own genealogy, shaped by caste, colonial history, and religious reform movements. Consider reading one South Asian feminist text alongside each Western one: the juxtaposition is more educating than either alone.
05

Identity, Diaspora & the Translated Self

Postcolonial Theory · Diaspora Studies · Philosophy of Identity
You crossed an ocean in 2014 and you carry at least two cultures in your nervous system. This module is about what that means — philosophically, psychologically, politically — and what unique forms of knowledge it produces.
Book
The Location of Culture
Homi K. Bhabha
Bhabha's concept of "the third space" — the hybrid space between cultures that migrants inhabit — is one of the most useful frameworks for understanding your own experience. Dense academic prose, but the concepts are worth the effort.
Book
The Namesake
Jhumpa Lahiri
Fiction as phenomenology. Lahiri renders the interior experience of the Bengali diaspora — the grief and freedom of translation — more precisely than most academic texts. Read it as philosophy, not as comfort.
Book
The Wretched of the Earth
Frantz Fanon
Fanon on the psychological violence of colonialism — how it is internalized, reproduced, and resisted. Chapter 5, "Colonial War and Mental Disorders," is a devastating account of how political structures damage psyches. Foundational for understanding South Asia's modern history.
Book
Orientalism
Edward Said
Said's account of how the West constructed "the East" as its exotic, irrational other — and how that construction served imperial power. Read the introduction and Part I at minimum. Then notice where you have unconsciously adopted the Western gaze toward your own culture.
Essay
"Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988)
Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak
Spivak, a Bengali feminist theorist, asks whether those who have been systematically silenced by colonialism can make themselves heard — and on whose terms. Difficult, important, and unexpectedly personal once you sit with it. Widely available online.
Podcast
Desi Standard Time
Available on Spotify and Apple Podcasts
Thoughtful conversations about South Asian diaspora identity — negotiating tradition and modernity, caste, queerness, mental health. Less academic than the readings above, but an important voice from inside the community.
The connecting thread The experience of being "in translation" between cultures is not merely a personal circumstance — it is an epistemological position. Philosophers like Bhabha and Spivak argue it produces a form of double vision that is analytically powerful. You have been doing theory without knowing it.
06

How to Think — Philosophy as Practice

Philosophy · Epistemology · Ethics · Logic
This module is less about philosophy's content and more about what philosophy does — how it sharpens thinking, surfaces hidden assumptions, and gives you tools to interrogate ideas rather than just receive them. These are books you return to, not books you finish.
Book
Sophie's World
Jostein Gaarder
A novel that is also a history of Western philosophy. An unusual entry point — but if you haven't had a formal philosophy education, this covers the arc from Socrates to Sartre with unusual elegance. A map before the territory.
Book
Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid
Douglas Hofstadter
A Pulitzer Prize-winning exploration of self-reference, consciousness, and the nature of mind — through mathematics, music, and visual art. For someone with your combination of interests (music, tech, consciousness), this is essential. Start with the dialogues.
Book
Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge and the Teachings of Plants
Robin Wall Kimmerer
A botanist and member of the Potawatomi Nation argues that Western science and indigenous epistemologies illuminate different truths — and that we need both. Deeply relevant to the spirituality-science tension running through your interests. Beautiful prose.
Book
Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things: What Categories Reveal About the Mind
George Lakoff
Lakoff's cognitive linguistics shows that the categories we think in are not neutral — they are embodied, culturally shaped, and ideologically loaded. How we categorize is how we see. Essential foundation for both tech ethics and feminist theory.
Book
Being and Time (selections)
Martin Heidegger
Don't read it all — read Division I (on being-in-the-world and everydayness) and Heidegger's concept of Dasein. His analysis of "thrownness" — finding yourself already in a world you did not choose, with a language you did not pick — is extraordinarily resonant for a diasporic experience.
Video
Philosophy Bites Podcast / YouTube Series
Nigel Warburton & David Edmonds
Short (15–20 min), rigorous interviews with major philosophers on single topics. Start with episodes on: personal identity (Derek Parfit), moral luck (Thomas Nagel), and the epistemology of testimony. Free, vast archive.
07

Synthesis — The Threads That Run Through Everything

Cross-disciplinary · Capstone Reading
These are texts that refuse to stay in one discipline. They are for after you've worked through the other modules — but many are good entry points too.
Book
The Structure of Scientific Revolutions
Thomas Kuhn
Kuhn's concept of "paradigm shifts" — that science doesn't progress linearly but through revolutionary breaks — applies to every field in this syllabus. Essential for understanding why it is so hard to think outside the frameworks you were trained in.
Book
Ways of Seeing
John Berger
Starts from painting, ends at advertising, and rewires how you look at images, power, and gender in between. The chapter on how women are depicted — "men act, women appear" — is four decades old and has not aged a day.
Book
Atlas of AI
Kate Crawford
Crawford maps the full material, political, and labor infrastructure behind AI systems — from lithium mines to data center workers to military contracts. The counterpart to Zuboff: where Zuboff is about data as capital, Crawford is about AI as empire.
Book
The Dispossessed
Ursula K. Le Guin
Science fiction as philosophy. A physicist moves between two societies built on opposing values — one of property, one of radical mutual aid. Le Guin does ethics through worldbuilding with more sophistication than most moral philosophy. Read this one slowly.
Music
M.S. Subbulakshmi — Bhaja Govindam & Vishnu Sahasranama
Various recordings, 1960s–90s
A woman who became the custodian of a devotional tradition not designed for her gender — and transformed it. Listen as a feminist and musicologist, not just a devotee. What does it mean to inhabit a tradition as a woman? What does she give, what does she refuse?
Website
The Marginalian (formerly Brain Pickings)
Maria Popova — themarginalian.org
Popova curates connections between literature, science, philosophy, and art with unusual intelligence. Use as a weekly reading supplement rather than a primary source — but her essay archives on consciousness, creativity, and meaning are genuinely rich.
Website
Aeon Magazine — aeon.co
Multiple contributors
The best general-readership home for rigorous ideas across philosophy, psychology, science, and culture. Free, browser-based, searchable. A reliable source of quality long-form essays across almost every topic in this syllabus.

A note on how to use this

A syllabus implies sequence, but learning this kind doesn't work that way. Follow what pulls you. If Heidegger stops you cold, go read Kimmerer. If a paper cites something you've never heard of, follow the citation. The goal is not completion — it is the development of a mind that finds connections everywhere it looks.

Your position — a South Indian woman who practices Hindustani classical music and builds technology in Silicon Valley — is not incidental to this curriculum. It is the curriculum's subject. The most interesting questions are the ones only you, from where you stand, can ask.
